Page 4 - What is ARM, and Why its Twisting the Industry's Arm

ARM, or Advanced RISC Machines, was designed by the British company ARM Holdings. In 2013, ARM became the most widely used 32-bit instruction set architecture in the world. ARM Holdings does not manufacture its CPUs however. Instead, it chooses to license its CPU designs to manufacturers. ARM Holdings profits off of licenses it sells and earns royalties on the units that use their CPUs. As of 2010 ARM CPUs were used in 95% of smartphones, 35% of televisions and 10% of mobile computers.

What makes ARM so special?

ARM CPUs have 3 main design features that allow have increased performance over other processors.

  1. ARM processors implement the RISC architecture; this means that ARM cores are much simpler than general-purpose processors. Due to the simpler design, the chip contains fewer transistors and has more room for application-specific macrocells. This reduces the amount of power the CPU requires and makes the chip less expensive to produce.

  2. The ARM ISA and RISC architecture are aimed at minimizing energy consumption; this is very important for embedded systems because they run on batteries and power is limited.

  3. ARM is a modular architecture, which means that the only requirement for the architecture is integer pipelining. Features such as memory management, floating point operations, and caching are all optional. Therefore, ARM gives way to a lot of flexibility when it comes to developing application-specific, ARM-based processors.

ARM's chip design not only allows the processor to be smaller and consume less power than general purpose processors, it also provides incredible performance for embedded systems. An example that illustrates this idea is the PXA255 XScale processor that runs at 400MHz and provides comparable performance to Pentium 2 running at 300MHz. However, the PXA255 processor uses 50 times less energy than the Pentium 2 processor.
