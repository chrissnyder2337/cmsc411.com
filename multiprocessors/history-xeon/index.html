History of Xeon

Intel Corporation started in 1968, and mostly developed SRAM and DRAM memory chips until 1981. They developed the first commercial microprocessor chip in 1971, but they didn't shift their focus to microprocessor designs until the 1990s, at which point they became the most prominent supplier and designer of microprocessors.

The ISAs used in Intel microprocessors are part of x86. The first member in the x86 family was a 16-bit architecture. Later, x86 would also consist of 32-bit and 64-bit architectures, called x86-32 or IA-32 and x86-64 or EM64T respectively

In 1981, the company started direct its work toward microprocessors, and created the 386 microprocessor. This high-performance chip brought Intel to a very strong position in the market. In 1989, Intel would follow up on the 386 by releasing the 486, which was stronger and notable for being the first RISC-like pipelined x86 design as well as the first x86 chip to use more than a million transistors. The next releases would be the Pentium in 1993, the Pentium Pro in 1995, and the Pentium II in 1997. The first Xeon chip was developed in 1998.

1998 Onwards:

 

1998 to 1999:

Pentium II Xeon "Drake"

The first series of Xeon processors. These were high-end versions of the "Deschutes" Pentium II core.

The Xeon version of "Deschutes" achieved larger cache sizes by putting additional 512 KB chips on the board.

 

Frequency (MHz)

 400 - 450
L2 Cache 512 KB- 2 MB

1999 to 2001: Pentium III Xeon "Tanner," "Cascades"

The first Pentium III Xeon series, codenamed Tanner, was based off of the Pentium III "Katmai." Both Tanner and Katmai were significant improvements over their predecessors.

Frequency (MHz)

 500 - 550
L2 Cache 512 KB- 2 MB
The next two rounds of Pentium III Xeon were both codenamed "Cascades." These were based on the Pentium III "Coppermine" core. Frequency (MHz) 600 - 1000
L2 Cache 256 KB- 2 MB

2001

After the Pentium III Xeon series, Xeon would be given its own brand and would no longer have "Pentium in the name. The next Xeon chips would still be based off of Pentium 4 and Pentium D, however.

2001:

“Foster” and “Foster MP”

Foster performed worst than the old Pentium III Xeon processors, as well as its AMD equivalent, the Athlon MP chip.

Frequency (MHz)

 1400-2000
L2 Cache 256 KB
This second version of Foster had multi-processing capabilities, but brought an insignificant improvment to the original Foster. Frequency (MHz) 1400-1600
L2 Cache 256 KB
L3 Cache 512 KB- 1 MB

2002 to 2004: “Prestonia” and “Gallatin”

Prestonia was based off of the "Northwood" Pentium 4 core. It used Intel's new Hyper-Threading Technology. These performed better than Foster, Cascades, and Athlon MP CPUs.

Frequency (MHz)

 1600-3066
L2 Cache 512 KB
Gallatin was essentially the MP version of Prestonia, meaning it was Prestonia with multiprocessing capabilities. Frequency (MHz) 2400-3200
L2 Cache 512 KB

2003:

Opteron, Xeon's Competition

The first of AMD's Opteron series were produced and would become Xeon's main competition. In addition, AMD and Intel both developed 64-bit extensions to x86 called x86-64 and Intel 64 respectively.

2004 to 2005: “Nocona,” “Irwindale,” “Cranford,” and “Potomac”

All of these chips were based off of Pentium 4 "Prescott." Nocona was notably slower than AMD's Opteron.

 2800-3600
L2 Cache 1 MB
Irwindale was a slight improvement over Nocona, but was still outperformed by its AMD counterpart. Frequency (MHz) 2800-3800
L2 Cache 2 MB
Cranford was a cheap MP version of Nocona. Frequency (MHz) 3166-3667
L2 Cache 1 MB
Potomac was a more expensive and powerful version of Cranford. Frequency (MHz) 2833-3333
L2 Cache 1 MB
L3 Cache 4 MB - 8 MB
