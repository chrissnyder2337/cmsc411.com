Page 3 - Popular RISC Based Architectures, FLOPS and Successes

In this section, we discuss the hits and misses for RISC-based architectures throughout history. We mainly concentrate on the successes and more importantly, the architectures that rival ARM today.

Nonetheless, not many well-known RISC architectures have failed to garner success in the market throughout time. However, the m88k is an example of a RISC architecture that did not succeed after its release.

Flops

Motorola 88k: The m88k, also known as the Motorola 88000, is an ISA which employes 32 32-bit registers and 32 80-bit floating point registers. Developed in the early 1980’s, this proprietary ISA was attractive in that it used separate instruction and data caches as well as separate data and address buses. By having separate buses and caches, this design allowed for increased data throughput. By having instructions and data move independently, there were fewer structural hazards and resource conflicts, making data flow through the pipe faster. However, the downside to this ISA was that it used the same register file for integer and floating point operations. Floating point operations take significantly more time to complete than integer operations and therefore performance suffered. Having to stall a quick integer operation for a lengthy floating-point operation was why the m88k ISA was not as successful as other RISC ISAs. The m88k was a commercial flop not only because of its design flaws, but that it was released two years after SPARC and MIPS took hold in the computing industry.

And now, we go on to the successes.

Successes

MIPS64 - R4000 - Released in 1991, this ISA was one of the first 64-bit architectures that utilized an eight stage pipeline as opposed to previous MIPS architectures which employed a five stage pipeline. Employing the eight stage pipeline had such a significant impact on performance due to the fact that fewer stalls were needed and as a result higher clock rates were achieved. The eight stage pipeline coupled with a 64-bit instruction set, 64-bit registers, and large caches made this architecture a commercial success.

POWER - Power stands for Performance Optimization with Enhanced RISC.  It is a microprocessor architecture first developed by by IBM in 1990.  All instructions, other than floating point instructions, are completed in one clock cycle.  POWER utilizes 32 32-bit integer registers and 32 64-bit floating point registers.  The branch unit also has access to its own private registers, which includes the program counter.  POWER also includes a virtual address feature which maps all addresses into an allotted 52 bits of space.  This allows for applications to share 32 bits and also have their own 32 bits. The main reason for POWER’s ISA commercial success is that it had the capability to do 64-bit multiply and divide operations in one clock cycle. Also, the ability to send data to the ALU and FPU simultaneously made POWER one of the first superscalar designs.

SPARC V8 - In 1990, SPARC International developed the next iteration of its SPARC architectures. Prior to SPARC V8, SPARC International’s architecture lacked integer multiply and divide capability. With the new iteration, engineers added the missing multiply and divide operations as well as a larger floating-point arithmetic capability. What makes SPARC V8 stand out from most RISC architectures is that it contained a 128-bit extended precision floating-point arithmetic capability. SPARC V8 architecture was so innovative that it served as the basis for IEEE Standard 1754-1994.
