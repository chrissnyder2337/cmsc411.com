This assumption is not true on a pipelined processor. A situation where the expected result is problematic is a hazard. Imagine the following two register instructions to a hypothetical RISC processor:</p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;">1: Add 1 to R5.<br />2: Copy R5 to R6.</p> <p>If the processor has the 5 steps listed in the initial illustration, instruction 1 would be fetched at time<em> t1</em> and its execution would be complete at <em>t5</em>. Instruction 2 would be fetched at <em>t2</em> and would be complete at <em>t6</em>. The first instruction might deposit the incremented number into R5 as its fifth step (register write back) at <em>t5</em>. But the second instruction might get the number from R5 (to copy to R6) in its second step (instruction decode and register fetch) at time <em>t3</em>. So instruction 2 is fetching an out-of-date value in its decode stage, which may result in a incorrect outcome. There are 3 different types of hazards:</p> <p><strong>Data Hazards</strong></p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;">Data hazards occur when instructions that exhibit data dependence, modify data in different stages of a pipeline. There are three situations in which a data hazard can occur:<br />For the following, consider two instructions i1 and i2, with i1 occurring before i2 in program order.</p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;"><strong>Read After Write (RAW)</strong><br />A RAW hazard occurs when instruction i2 tries to read a source before i1 writes to it. A RAW data hazard refers to a situation where an instruction refers to a result that has not yet been calculated or retrieved. This is exactly what is happening in the example above. </p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;"><strong>Write After Read (WAR)</strong><br />A WAR data hazard occurs when instuction i2 tries to write a destination before it is read by i1. A WAR data hazard represents a problem with concurrent execution. However, for our exmaple 5-stage pipeline, a WAR hazard cannot occur.<br />For example:</p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;"><img alt="" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?i1:\%20R4%20\xleftarrow\%20R1%20+%20R3" style="width: 154px; height: 15px;" /></p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;"><img alt="" src="http://latex.codecogs.com/gif.latex?i2:\%20R3%20\xleftarrow\%20R1%20+%20R2" style="width: 154px; height: 15px;" /><br />If we are in a situation that there is a chance that i2 may be completed before i1 (i.e. with out-of-order execution) we must ensure that we do not store the result of register R3 before i1 has had a chance to fetch the operands.</p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;"><strong>Write After Write (WAW)</strong><br />A WAW hazard occurs when an instruction i2 tries to write an operand before it is written by i1. A WAW data hazard may occur in a concurrent execution environment. However, once again, it cannot occur in our simple example of a 5-stage pipeline. </p> <p><strong>Structural Hazards</strong></p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;">A structural hazard occurs when a part of the processor's hardware is needed by two or more instructions at the same time. Going back to our laundry example, imagine that this time there is only one person who both gathers the dirty clothes for the next load and folds the clothes after they come out of the dryer. Assuming each of these tasks take the time of a complete cycle, both tasks cannot be done in the same cycle. Thus, a load will have to wait until that person is free to gather the dirty clothes again, which introduces a wait into the pipeline. This wait in the pipeline is reffered to as a <strong>pipeline stall</strong> (or pipeline bubble). </p> <p><strong>Control Hazards</strong></p> <p style="margin-left: 40px;">Control hazards occur with branches. On many instruction pipeline microarchitectures, the processor will not know the outcome of the branch (after the EX stage) when it needs to insert a new instruction into the pipeline (the IF stage).</p> </div></div></div> Thu, 02 May 2013 23:32:51 +0000 bradyo 97 at http://cmsc411.com http://cmsc411.com/pipelining-techniques/hazards#comments References http://cmsc411.com/hardware-pipelining-techniques-software/references <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"><ul><li> <p dir="ltr" style="line-height: 1.15; margin-top: 0pt; margin-bottom: 0pt;"><b id="docs-internal-guid-36f99c24-6d6e-0ab1-dd11-ed3aaa0b8edd" style="font-family: 'Times New Roman'; font-size: medium; line-height: normal; font-weight: normal;"><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial; background-color: transparent; white-space: pre-wrap;">Cebenoyan, Cem. “Chapter 28.
